Designing Data Exploration

It is 1990, just a couple of weeks after the Berlin wall came down. It's an exciting time in Germany.
The wall is gone. Yet, the people of Eastern Germany still live in the GDR, behind the iron curtain. But they are no longer afraid of the oppressive Eastern German state and its infamous Secret Police, the Stasi.
Some context: In 1989 the Stasi had 280.000 employees, official ones but also unofficial ones, spying on their own citizens and fighting the Eastern German opposition in secret.
But now it becomes clear that the days of the Stasi are over. As the Stasi starts to secretly destroy their data, protesters occupy the Stasi headquarters.
Eventually, citizen committees are formed to watch over the termination of the Stasi. And there is a huge public discussion on what to do with the Stasis heritage – both in terms of data and physical properties.

Meet my colleague David Crawford, now a senior reporter at CORRECTIV. He is also a hoarder of government documents. In the picture, he is posing with a massive trove of secret service files. This is exactly his thing.

In 1990, David was a freelance journalist specializing in what people back then called computer assisted reporting.

In the chaos of Eastern Berlin of 1990, David gets hold of a data set containing listings of several thousand Stasi properties all across the country. Offices, apartments, but also sports facilities, hotels, restaurants, even a camp site – all used by the Stasi, sometimes openly, but mostly in secret.

David realizes how important this information for the citizens of the GDR, and he decides to get this published. Not just the story, but the whole dataset.

He gets Western German newspaper Taz to publish a special issue consisting mainly of long lists of properties, sorted by city and post codes, so that people can find the former Stasi properties in their neighborhood.

Reason: Taz is the only western German newspaper that is broadly available on Eastern German newsstands.

The issue is such a success that, after it sells out on newsstands, taz makes it available via mail-order. Today, a copy can be found in the German national archive and, if you ever come to Berlin, you can see another copy in the Stasi museum.

Like my co-worker David, many Journalists, scientists, public authorities are literally sitting on large data sets. This is what we work with.

The final graphics or charts that we eventually publish as parts of news pieces or reports are high level summaries of the data we work with. They are the result of analysis, not the actual data itself. Very often this makes sense. After all, it is your job to analyze the data, identify the relevant aspects, and find the ways to show them. You decide what’s interesting or relevant and prepare it for your audience.
(Screenshot care homes)
But sometimes, its the individual data points that are interesting for your audience, not just the big picture. This is a screenshot of a project I worked on last year. It makes data from the inspection reports of 13,000 care homes in Germany available to the public. It is what we call a news app or a data explorer. Obviously, the web is a much better tool for publishing large data sets than a newspaper.
  • Provide tools for searching, querying, browsing
  • The web has no character limit, you can publish all the data + all the information you need to explain the data and put individual data points into context
(Screenshot ICIJ data explorer)
Here you see the ICIJ offshore leaks database. It makes data [what exactly] from the panama papers leak and other ICIJ investigations accessible to the public. This is an example of another reason to make the data you are sitting on publicly accessible. By doing so, you enable others to build upon your work.

Tools
not graphics

  • Complexity of interacting with data moved to the user
  • We build tools, but not generic one like R-Studio or Excel
  • Domain-specific data visualization tools
  • specific to a single data set
  • constraints: while you want to give access to all the data, you also don’t want your users to get lost. You have to guide users to find relevant or interesting information in the data. You have to make some decisions for your users.
