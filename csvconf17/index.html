CORRECTIV, investigative journalism non-profit from Germany. We do public interest journalism often based on data, very much like Propublica here in the US.
It is 1990, just a couple of weeks after the Berlin wall came down.
The wall is gone. Yet, the people of Eastern Germany still live in the GDR, behind the iron curtain. But they are no longer afraid of the oppressive Eastern German state and its infamous Secret Police, the Stasi.
Some context: The Stasi was a massive spying operation mostly targetting the citizens of Germany. The Stasi had 280.000 employees, official ones but also unofficial ones, spying on citizens and fighting the Eastern German opposition in secret.
But in 1990 it becomes clear that the days of the Stasi are over. As the Stasi starts to secretly destroy their data, protesters occupy the Stasi headquarters.
Eventually, citizen committees are formed to watch over the termination of the Stasi. And there is a huge public discussion on what to do with the Stasis heritage – both in terms of data and physical property.

Meet my colleague David Crawford, now a senior reporter at CORRECTIV. David is also a hoarder of government documents. In the picture, he is posing with a massive trove of secret service files. This is his thing.

In 1990, way before CORRECTIV existed, David was a freelance journalist specializing in what people call computer assisted reporting.

In the chaos of Eastern Berlin of 1990, David gets hold of a data set containing listings of several thousand Stasi properties all across the country. Offices, apartments, but also sports facilities, hotels, restaurants, even a camp site – all used by the Stasi, sometimes openly, but mostly in secret.

David realizes how important this information for the citizens of Eastern Germany, and he decides to get this published. Not just the story, but the whole dataset.

He gets Western German newspaper Taz to publish a special issue consisting mainly of long lists of properties, sorted by city and post codes, so that people can find the former Stasi properties in their neighborhood.

The issue is such a success that, after it sells out on newsstands, taz makes it available via mail-order. Today, a copy can be found in the German national archive and, if you ever come to Berlin, you can see another copy in the Stasi museum.

Fast forward 27 years. Like my coworker David, many Journalists, scientists, public authorities are literally sitting on large data sets. This is what we work with.

Exploring Data

When we analyze data using tools like Jupiter Notebooks, R, Tableau, or Excel, we usually start with exploring it. Either with formalized statistical methods or just by browsing and looking at it from different angles.

The final graphics or charts that we eventually publish as parts of news pieces or reports are high level summaries of the data we work with. They are the result of analysis, not the actual data itself. Very often this makes sense.

After all, it is your job to analyze the data, identify the relevant aspects, and find the ways to show them. You decide what’s interesting or relevant and prepare it for your audience.

But sometimes, its the individual data points that are interesting for your audience, not just the big picture.

This is a screenshot of a project I worked on last year. We call this a news app or a data explorer. This news app makes data from the inspection reports of 13,000 care homes in Germany available to the public. It is a companion app to our reporting on issues in the care sector.

What we do here is, we generate a short article for every single nursing home in Germany, based on the data from the official inspection reports – but in a way that is actually usable by a normal person.

ICIJ offshore leaks database. It makes data from the panama papers leak and other ICIJ investigations accessible to the public. Journalists across the world are using this to investigate money laundering and tax evasion in their respective countries.

This is an example of another reason to make the data you are sitting on publicly accessible. By doing so, you enable others to build upon your work.

Obviously, the web is a much better tool for publishing large data sets than a newspaper.

  • The web has no character limit
  • you can publish all the data + explain it to and put individual data points into context
  • Interaction: Simple tooltip
  • provide tools for searching, querying, browsing

Tools
not graphics

  • Complexity of interacting with data moved to the audience
  • We build tools, but not generic ones like R-Studio or Excel
  • Data visualization tools that are specific to a single data set
  • Constraints: while you want to give access to all the data, you also don’t want your users to get lost. You have to guide users to find relevant or interesting information in the data. So you have to make some decisions for your users.

Another aspect to what: Ethics

  • Is there public interest in this?
  • Does this infringe on someones privacy?
  • Anonymizing data: Redacting names is often not enough

Who?

  • goals/interest in the topic
  • data literacy
  • media consumption habits
In Davids case, his target group was the Public of eastern Germany. So he choose a newspaper that was widely available across the state. But most probably, The Public is not your target group.
Related to this is also: What is the environment this tool will be used in. Will it be casually, maybe on mobile, with low attention.
Or will people use it more purposeful and with high attention, maybe in an office environment.

How?

Macro: Far view

  • The big picture
  • High-level overviews

Micro: Near view

  • Individual data points
  • Anecdotes or stories

A traditional approach storytelling in data journalism is to look at the big picture, use some numbers from this in your reporting, and then look for an anecdote or two to tell a story.
